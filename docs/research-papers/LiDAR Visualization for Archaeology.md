# **State of the Art in LiDAR-Derived Terrain Visualization for Archaeological Prospection: Algorithms, Benchmarks, and Adoption Trends (2018–2025)**

## **The Paradigm Shift in Geomorphometric Prospection**

The integration of Airborne Laser Scanning (ALS), universally referred to as LiDAR, into the discipline of archaeological prospection has fundamentally transformed the detection, mapping, and morphological analysis of anthropogenic landscapes. In the nascent stages of LiDAR application, the archaeological community relied almost exclusively on simple analytical hillshading. However, this rudimentary technique is severely hampered by directional bias, wherein structural anomalies aligned parallel to the simulated light source remain invisible, and extreme shadowing entirely obscures the bottom of deep valleys or steep karstic depressions.1 Consequently, the discipline has undergone a paradigm shift toward multi-scalar, isotropic, and complex composite visualization techniques.  
Between 2018 and 2025, the global proliferation of high-resolution digital terrain models (DTMs)—driven by monumental survey efforts such as the Pacunam Lidar Initiative in Guatemala, the Greater Angkor Project in Cambodia, and statewide open-data initiatives in Europe—spurred the rapid development of advanced geomorphometric algorithms.3 These algorithms are meticulously designed to extract increasingly subtle, micro-topographic anomalies that are otherwise obscured by dense tropical canopy, degraded by millennia of intensive agrarian ploughing, or hidden within the complex macro-topography of mountainous regions.3  
The transition from single-parameter visualizations to complex algorithmic compositing reflects the growing necessity to isolate discrete archaeological features—such as charcoal kilns, Maya plazuelas, ancient agrarian terraces, and eroded medieval hollow ways—from natural geologic baselines.4 While foundational algorithms such as the Sky-View Factor (SVF), Topographic Openness, Simple Local Relief Models (SLRM), and Multi-Scale Topographic Position (MSTP) remain cornerstones of remote sensing workflows, the contemporary literature demonstrates a broad adoption of highly sophisticated derivative models.8 These modern techniques focus on optimizing visual contrast, mathematically mitigating computational noise directly within the raster arrays, and facilitating automated feature detection pipelines using convolutional neural networks (CNNs).2  
This comprehensive report provides an exhaustive analysis of the state of the art in LiDAR-derived terrain visualization from 2018 to 2025\. It evaluates novel algorithms introduced to the archaeological literature, defines optimal parameter values across diverse environmental contexts, benchmarks leading software implementations, maps regional workflow paradigms, and critically assesses the integration of these visualizations into machine learning architectures. The insights contained herein are designed to inform the continuous development of open-source geographic information system (GIS) environments.

## **Advanced Visualization Algorithms in Contemporary Literature**

Since 2018, several advanced visualization algorithms have transitioned from the experimental fringes of geomatics into standard archaeological practice. These algorithms seek to overcome the limitations of foundational methods by fusing trend removal, diffuse illumination proxies, and localized slope dynamics into highly saturated, multivariate outputs. For developers of open-source spatial toolsets, integrating these methods requires a nuanced understanding of their underlying mathematics, rendering behaviors, and intellectual property constraints.

### **Red Relief Image Map (RRIM) and Simple Red Relief**

The Red Relief Image Map (RRIM) represents a watershed moment in archaeological visualization. Originally conceptualized and patented by Chiba et al. (2008), the RRIM achieved widespread prominence in archaeological prospection following its highly successful deployment in the Maya lowlands by Inomata et al. (2017) at the site of Ceibal, Guatemala.11 By design, RRIM is a composite visualization that represents topographic slope as a chromatic red gradient and topographic openness as a greyscale brightness gradient.11 The resulting output renders steep slopes in vivid red, localized depressions in dark tones, elevated ridges in bright white, and flat terrains as neutral gray.11  
The fundamental advantage of RRIM lies in its ability to highlight sharp morphological breaks—such as the edges of platform mounds, the walls of defensive earthworks, or the steep sides of sinkholes—without the severe directional bias inherent to traditional analytical hillshading.11 Recent iterations of the proprietary RRIM logic also incorporate the Enhanced Visualization Model (EVM) and Colored Relief Image Maps (CRIM). These advanced iterations infuse subtle blue-green tints into topographic concavities to separate them further from flat terrain matrices, while rendering convexities in bright yellow.11  
However, because the original RRIM algorithm and its specific formulaic combinations are patented, its direct integration into open-source platforms like QGIS or Python libraries has historically faced substantial legal and technical barriers.11 To circumvent this limitation, the open-source archaeological community developed a highly effective, patent-free analogue termed "Simple Red Relief".11 Simple Red Relief replaces the proprietary openness computation with a carefully parameterized Simple Local Relief Model (SLRM) overlaid by a standard slope raster.11  
In a standard GIS environment, the Simple Red Relief composite is implemented through a precise layered rendering technique. The lower layer consists of an SLRM computed with a trend-assessment radius calibrated to the grid resolution of the DTM (typically 12 to 20 meters). A custom color ramp is applied to the SLRM, assigning cyan to depressions (negative values), grey to flat terrain, and pale yellow to convexities.11 The upper layer consists of a slope raster rendered from 0 degrees (white) to a maximum saturation threshold—typically 50 degrees—in vivid red.11 The upper layer's blend mode is set to "Multiply" over the SLRM, dynamically rendering a patent-free visualization that is functionally indistinguishable from the proprietary RRIM.11

### **Local Dominance (LD)**

Local Dominance (LD) is a profoundly useful algorithm that computes the average steepness of the angle at which an idealized observer, positioned at a defined height above a given pixel, looks down upon the surrounding surface.14 The algorithm generates high output values for pixels situated on local elevations and steep slopes, while yielding low values for pixels situated in local topographic depressions.14  
Implemented frequently in Python via high-performance array calculations, LD operates as a sophisticated, angular alternative to the SLRM.15 While the SLRM calculates absolute relative elevation anomalies by subtracting a smoothed trend surface from the original DTM, Local Dominance is fundamentally based on angular relationships.14 Consequently, LD successfully retains a generalized visual impression of the macroscopic landscape form because it naturally produces higher values on natural geological slopes than on flat horizontal planes.14  
In the context of archaeological prospection, LD possesses immense genuine utility for identifying exceedingly low-relief features in flat or moderately undulating terrain.14 It has rapidly become a standard approach in European archaeology for extracting leveled burial mounds, highly eroded ancient field boundaries, and subtle mining traces—such as dolines and hollow ways—that are otherwise completely absorbed or distorted by standard SLRM detrending.14

### **Anisotropic Sky-View Factor (ASVF)**

While the standard Sky-View Factor (SVF) evaluates the proportion of the visible sky hemisphere from a target pixel assuming uniform, isotropic illumination, the Anisotropic Sky-View Factor (ASVF) modifies this assumption by weighting the sky illumination directionally.16 ASVF applies a cosine function of the half-angle to simulate an anisotropic sky dome where illumination is significantly brighter from a specific user-defined azimuth.2  
The ASVF algorithm occupies a critical analytical middle ground between diffuse SVF and harsh multi-directional hillshade.17 It retains the diffuse, shadow-penetrating characteristics of the standard SVF—preventing deep linear trenches or steep valleys from disappearing into total darkness—while simultaneously introducing a subtle directional contrast that mimics natural solar illumination.17 In practice, ASVF enhances the structural legibility of small, low-relief features on flat terrain much more sharply than standard SVF.17 Implementations of ASVF typically require three parameters: the azimuth of highest weight (usually set to 315 degrees), the exponent defining the gradient of anisotropy, and the minimal possible weight (which dictates the base level of ambient light).16

### **Prismatic Openness and Enhanced Prismatic Openness**

Prismatic Openness was formally introduced to the literature by Canuto et al. (2018) during the publication of the massive Pacunam Lidar Initiative in Guatemala, designed specifically to facilitate the rapid visual detection of small-scale Maya house mounds and agricultural terraces.3 The original method applied a drastic 3x vertical exaggeration to the raw DTM, subsequently computing multi-directional hillshading from three specific angles to create a "prismatic" relief effect that artificially steepened subtle architectural remains.3  
Because rigid vertical exaggeration often heavily distorts natural terrain and renders geometric measurements inaccurate, Verbovšek et al. (2019) subsequently developed *Enhanced Prismatic Openness*.3 This revised algorithm completely removes the problematic vertical exaggeration step. Instead, it utilizes an overlay fusion technique (blend mode compositing) that mathematically integrates Positive Openness and Negative Openness with three-directional shaded relief.3 The result is a highly saturated, multi-chromatic image where structural edges are vividly pronounced without warping the underlying topography.3 Within reference toolkits, Enhanced Prismatic Openness serves as a primary tool for visually segregating micro-topography in environments where slope alone provides insufficient contrast for the human eye.3

### **Enhanced Multi-Scale Topographic Position (eMSTP Variants)**

The standard Multi-Scale Topographic Position (MSTP) calculates localized elevation deviations across micro, meso, and macro scales, assigning these mathematical deviations to the blue, green, and red channels of an RGB composite.9 However, recognizing that MSTP alone can frequently appear visually noisy, lacking in intuitive depth, and difficult to interpret over large areas, researchers developed a suite of "enhanced" iterations between 2021 and 2025\.  
The first major revision, e2MSTP, fused the standard MSTP array with an SLRM and the patented RRIM to provide a clearer sense of overarching terrain.13 Because the RRIM component introduced patent uncertainty, the third iteration, e3MSTP, replaced the RRIM with a Colored Relief Image Map (CRIM) to avoid copyright infringement, utilizing complex color blending to highlight multi-scalar topography.13  
The most recent and exhaustive iteration is e4MSTP, finalized by Kokalj (2025). The e4MSTP algorithm abandons the RRIM framework entirely. Instead, it generates a highly complex composite by mathematically merging a red-toned slope layer, a combination of Positive and Negative Openness, Local Dominance, merged Sky-View Factors computed with two different search radii and distinct histogram stretches, and the original MSTP array.13  
The e4MSTP algorithm is uniquely powerful in highlighting minute topographical variations in entirely flat terrain, rendering it highly effective for detecting vast agricultural field systems and subtle structural footprints that span multiple spatial scales.13 In instances where the highly saturated colors of e4MSTP overwhelm the analyst or obscure vector mapping layers, the literature recommends applying a luminosity blend mode over a neutral background to strip the chrominance while retaining the razor-sharp structural edge data provided by the multi-scalar fusion.13

### **Visualization for Archaeological Topography (VAT)**

Perhaps the most widely adopted combinatorial algorithm since 2018 is the Visualization for Archaeological Topography (VAT), which offers a universally readable, standardized base map for landscape analysis.6 VAT is not a standalone mathematical calculation but rather a highly optimized fusion of four distinct, informative raster layers: Hillshade (or Multi-Hillshade), Slope, Positive Openness, and Sky-View Factor.6  
The fundamental design philosophy behind the development of VAT was to maintain the intuitive, natural readability of traditional hillshading while entirely eliminating its directional blind spots via the integration of isotropic algorithms.19 VAT is particularly praised for its geographic resilience; the visual extent and shape of archaeological features do not warp, distort, or become occluded when the analysis moves from flat alluvial plains to exceedingly steep upland karsts.6 The contemporary literature emphasizes that the *combined VAT* should ideally replace single-source analytical hillshading in all formal academic publications, as it provides substantially more morphometric detail while remaining free of algorithmic artifacts.19

### **Principal Component Analysis (PCA) Compositing**

PCA compositing is heavily utilized in European prospection, particularly in Italy and the United Kingdom, for the extraction of highly directional agrarian features.22 Rather than manually blending rasters using overlay modes, this approach calculates multidirectional hillshades—typically utilizing 16 to 32 discrete illumination azimuths around the 360-degree compass rose—and runs a Principal Component Analysis across the entire raster stack.22  
The first three principal components (PC1, PC2, PC3)—which contain the maximum variance in terrain illumination—are assigned to the Red, Green, and Blue channels of an output image.2 PCA compositing excels at capturing subtle anisotropic anomalies, such as faint plough marks, historical ridge-and-furrow systems, or the parallel flanking ditches of Roman roads, by isolating structural variance that occurs perpendicular to standard illumination geometries.7

## **Parameter Optimization Across Archaeological Contexts**

### **Parameterizing Flat and Agricultural Land**

In plains and alluvial contexts, natural topography changes gradually over massive distances. The prevailing rule of thumb for these environments is to employ significantly larger filter sizes and search radii.9 A large search radius (e.g., 20 meters for the SLRM trend assessment) ensures that the algorithm captures the true macro-topographic trend, preventing wide, subtle features—like Roman centuriation, sprawling Neolithic enclosures, or vast agricultural terraces—from being mistakenly absorbed into the smoothed baseline.11 In environments where modern mechanized ploughing has completely obliterated standing architecture, analysts rely heavily on Local Dominance. By setting the Observer Height to approximately 1.7 meters (simulating human eye level) and the Radial Distance to 10–20 pixels, the algorithm detects the exceedingly faint volumetric swelling of leveled burial mounds that cannot be perceived in standard slope or hillshade models.14

### **Parameterizing Forested Terrain and Dense Canopy**

While LiDAR's primary archaeological utility is its canopy penetration, filtering "ground" returns in heavily wooded areas often leaves significant micro-noise. This noise stems from low-lying vegetation, fallen trunks, boulders, and dense root systems that the classification algorithms fail to segregate from the true bare earth.9 For DTM generation in these environments, the literature highly recommends using "less aggressive" ground filtering routines (e.g., the archaeology \- fine setting in LAStools) to preserve near-surface structural returns, even at the cost of retaining some vegetation artifacts.9  
To mathematically accommodate the resulting noise in the DTM, visualizations like SVF must be calculated with tighter radii (e.g., 5 to 10 meters) and high algorithmic noise-removal settings to prevent the interpolation of false micro-pits and jagged artifacts.9 Positive Openness, operating at a tight 5-meter radius, is considered optimal in forests for tracing the convex ridges of standing masonry walls, as the small radius prevents the algorithm from being distracted by the broader slope of the forest floor.9

### **Parameterizing Upland and Steep Terrain**

In mountainous and steep karst landscapes, standard parameter configurations fail catastrophically because the sheer severity of macro-slopes overshadows micro-anthropogenic anomalies. In upland contexts, search radii for isotropic methods like SVF and Openness must be dramatically reduced, often to 5 meters or fewer.9 This restriction limits the algorithm's field of evaluation strictly to immediate neighboring pixels. If a larger radius (e.g., 20 meters) is used in steep terrain, the SVF algorithm interprets the entire side of a mountain as an obscured hemisphere, plunging the visualization into inky blackness and rendering any archaeological features invisible. The VAT algorithm is explicitly designed to handle upland terrain by neutralizing the over-saturation of steep slopes through its balanced blending of Positive Openness and localized slope dynamics.6

### **Parameterizing Coastal, Estuarine, and Bathymetric Terrain**

High-resolution bathymetry derived from LiDAR and multi-beam sonar is increasingly visualized using archaeological terrain algorithms.2 In these relatively flat but highly textured underwater environments, directional bias from analytical hillshading is highly detrimental, frequently erasing underwater sediment ripples, paleo-channels, or submerged maritime infrastructure.2 Because data resolution in aquatic environments is often slightly coarser (typically 1 to 2 meters) due to water attenuation and acoustic footprint size, larger pixel radii for multi-scalar algorithms are strictly required.2 For example, the macro scale of MSTP may need to evaluate up to 3000 pixels to identify large sandbanks, while SVF requires a 15-meter radius searching in 32 distinct directions to capture the smooth, undulating nature of the seabed without introducing angular artifacts.2

## **Software Architecture and Implementation Benchmarks**

The processing of LiDAR datasets is heavily dependent on the mathematical precision and computational efficiency of the underlying software architectures. The Relief Visualization Toolbox (RVT), maintained by the Slovenian Environment Agency and ZRC SAZU, currently functions as the global reference implementation for archaeological geomorphometry.2 RVT operates via a multifaceted deployment strategy: standalone executables for Windows, native plugins for QGIS, raster functions for ArcGIS Pro, and a highly optimized Python package (rvt.vis and rvt.blend) operating directly on multi-dimensional numpy arrays.15  
Understanding how RVT compares against other major geomatics platforms—specifically SAGA GIS, WhiteboxTools, and GRASS GIS—is essential for understanding adoption trends in the literature.

### **Benchmarking RVT against SAGA GIS**

Published benchmarks consistently highlight critical mathematical discrepancies between RVT and SAGA GIS, specifically regarding the calculation of the Sky-View Factor (SVF) and Anisotropic SVF.17 While SAGA GIS assumes a uniformly lit sky for its SVF implementation, it critically lacks a dedicated pre-processing module for micro-noise reduction.17 Consequently, in datasets containing inherent point-cloud noise or deep valley morphologies, SAGA GIS tends to produce highly saturated, visually "blown-out" outputs. In SAGA, valley bottoms are frequently rendered as homogeneous black zones, permanently destroying subtle archaeological details located within depressions.17  
Conversely, RVT implements a configurable noise-removal matrix (svf\_noise parameter: 0=none, 1=low, 2=medium, 3=high) directly within the SVF array calculation prior to final rendering.15 This mathematical intervention mitigates the saturation artifacts seen in SAGA GIS, rendering RVT vastly superior for extracting micromorphology in complex topographies.17 Furthermore, RVT natively supports Anisotropic SVF (ASVF), allowing analysts to explicitly dictate the azimuth and exponent of illumination weight via the asvf\_dir and asvf\_level parameters, a feature historically absent or highly cumbersome to execute in standard SAGA modules.16

### **Benchmarking RVT against WhiteboxTools and GRASS GIS**

WhiteboxTools and GRASS GIS represent robust, comprehensive geospatial environments that excel in broader geomorphometric classifications and hydrological modeling. For instance, the highly cited r.geomorphons module in GRASS GIS utilizes machine vision logic to categorize terrains into ten absolute geomorphological classes (e.g., flat, summit, ridge, valley, spur, hollow) based on line-of-sight algorithms. This strict categorization is highly efficient for the regional extraction of agrarian terraces or natural landforms.7  
However, WhiteboxTools and GRASS are fundamentally designed for physical geography and hydrology, rather than the highly specific aesthetic and morphometric requirements of micro-archaeological visualization. RVT maintains absolute dominance over these platforms in the archaeological sphere by packaging purely archaeological blends—such as VAT, e4MSTP, and Enhanced Prismatic Openness—into single-click GUI operations or single-script Python executions.2  
Crucially, RVT’s native integration of non-linear histogram stretching directly inside its blending functions (rvt.blend) ensures that researchers do not have to manually perform complex float-to-byte scaling, standard-deviation clips, and transparency adjustments across multiple separate raster channels, which is frequently required when using general-purpose tools like Whitebox.15

## **Regional Workflow Paradigms in Archaeological Practice**

The spatial scale of target archaeological features dictates the specific combinations of algorithms deployed in the field. A distinct methodological divergence exists between the standard workflows utilized in European archaeology and those applied in the vast, dense tropical environments of Mesoamerica and the Asia-Pacific.

### **The European Paradigm: Microtopography and Subdued Relief**

In European contexts, the vast majority of archaeological features are characterized by exceedingly limited vertical relief.27 The European landscape has been subjected to millennia of intense, continuous agrarian exploitation, urban expansion, and forestry. Consequently, prehistoric earthworks, Roman marching camps, and medieval field systems are predominantly embedded within the ground surface or leveled to less than a meter in height by historical ploughing.27  
The standard European workflow therefore heavily prioritizes trend-removal and micro-variance algorithms, executed in a specific sequential order 27:

1. **Macro-Detrending:** Analysts frequently begin by deploying a Simple Local Relief Model (SLRM) using a large search radius (15-20m) to mathematically remove the dominant geologic slope, flattening the terrain to isolate localized deviations.14  
2. **Volumetric Peak Extraction:** Following the SLRM, Local Dominance (LD) is applied. By simulating an observer looking down upon the flattened terrain, LD is uniquely capable of locating the convex peaks of highly eroded barrows and the subtle concavities of infilled ditches that survive only as centimeters of relief.14  
3. **Anisotropic Delineation:** To trace the linear, highly directional striations of ploughed ridge-and-furrow systems or the parallel flanking ditches of ancient roads, PCA compositing of 16-direction hillshades is applied.7  
4. **Standardized Base Mapping:** Once discrete features are identified via the previous algorithms, the Visualisation for Archaeological Topography (VAT) is applied as the final basemap. VAT is considered the de facto standard across European heritage management, allowing for seamless visual comparison and standardized reporting between datasets from the United Kingdom, Italy, and Slovenia.6

### **The Mesoamerican and Asia-Pacific Paradigm: Dense Canopy and Monumentality**

In stark contrast to Europe, archaeological prospection in the Maya Lowlands and the Greater Angkor Region of Southeast Asia is defined by the presence of massive standing monumental architecture, complex urban plazuela groupings, and vast water-management earthworks hidden beneath some of the densest tropical rainforest canopies on the planet.5  
The primary challenge in these tropical regions is the sheer scale of the data—often covering thousands of square kilometers of continuous LiDAR capture—and the immediate need to visually segregate 10-meter-tall pyramids and sprawling causeways from adjacent natural limestone karsts and wetlands.3 The workflow sequence is tailored to macro-scale urban discovery:

1. **Macro-scale Urban Mapping:** Workflows in these regions heavily favor the Red Relief Image Map (RRIM), its patent-free derivatives (Simple Red Relief), and multi-scalar approaches like MSTP and e4MSTP.11 These algorithms forcefully color-code the terrain, enabling researchers to perform rapid, zoomed-out visual identification of massive plazuela groups, urban grids, and monumental epicenters across tens of kilometers.3  
2. **Volumetric Separation of Architecture:** Once urban centers are located, the Topographic Position Index (TPI) and SLRM are systematically utilized to identify the volumetric mass of individual platform mounds and domestic structures, separating them from the natural bedrock outcroppings.6  
3. **Hydrological and Defensive Tracing:** Finally, Negative Openness is specifically targeted and rendered in inverted greyscale to trace the deep concavities of extensive ancient reservoirs (such as the *barays* in Angkor or the *aguadas* in Mesoamerica) and defensive ditch networks that defined the borders of ancient polities.3

Because Mesoamerican and Southeast Asian archaeologists must rapidly classify hundreds of thousands of discrete architectural units over vast areas, chromatic multi-variate composites (like e4MSTP and RRIM) are vastly preferred over the greyscale micro-topographic arrays utilized in Europe.6

## **Machine Learning and Automated Feature Detection**

As the sheer volume of regional LiDAR data rapidly outpaces the capacity of manual human interpretation, the archaeological community has increasingly shifted toward automated feature detection using deep learning architectures—specifically Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs).4 The transition from legacy Object-Based Image Analysis (OBIA) to modern deep learning models requires a fundamental reimagining of how visualization algorithms are mathematically formatted as machine inputs.

### **Visualization Inputs for Deep Learning Models**

A standard CNN natively expects a three-band (RGB) optical image array as input. Feeding a raw, single-band 32-bit elevation raster into a CNN yields highly suboptimal results because raw elevation inherently lacks explicit morphological contrast.34 Therefore, researchers utilize advanced terrain visualizations as explicit feature-engineering layers, stacking discrete morphometric arrays into artificial RGB images.34  
A highly optimized and widely cited input configuration assigns the mathematical components of the VAT algorithm to individual color channels:

* **Red Channel:** Slope (highlighting geometric structural edges and sharp drop-offs).  
* **Green Channel:** Positive Openness (highlighting structural convexities, standing walls, and the rims of craters or mounds).  
* **Blue Channel:** Sky-View Factor (providing local contextual depth, shadow penetration, and background noise suppression).35

Alternatively, extracting multi-scalar parameters—such as the micro, meso, and macro components of the MSTP algorithm—directly into the RGB channels provides the CNN with immediate mathematical context regarding the object's spatial relation to its surrounding environment.9 This multi-band pre-processing step is absolutely critical; it forces the CNN's convolutional kernels to learn the explicit geometric "signatures" of archaeological structures (e.g., the perfect mathematical circularity of a burial mound in Positive Openness, or the parallel linear depressions of a trackway in Negative Openness) rather than attempting to infer them from raw elevation shifts.33

### **Algorithms and Deep Learning Architectures**

The dominant deep learning architectures utilized in archaeological detection are currently semantic segmentation networks and instance segmentation frameworks.

* **Mask R-CNN:** Extensively applied for instance segmentation, Mask R-CNN excels at placing precise bounding boxes and pixel-level polygon masks over discrete, isolated objects. It is the prevailing architecture for detecting charcoal kilns, burial mounds, and individual Maya platform structures in heavily forested terrain.36  
* **Deeplab V3+:** Utilized heavily for semantic segmentation, this architecture classifies every individual pixel in the image. It is highly favored for mapping continuous, sprawling features that do not possess neat boundaries, such as the vast ancient reservoir networks and agrarian field systems of the Greater Angkor Region.5  
* **Transfer Learning (TL):** Because meticulously annotated archaeological ground-truth datasets are exceedingly small relative to standard computer vision datasets, Transfer Learning is universally deployed. Models pre-trained on massive optical datasets (e.g., ResNet-50 or SatCLIP) have their final network weights fine-tuned exclusively on the artificially colored LiDAR composites (like VAT or RRIM).7

### **Validation Metrics and Ground-Truth Dependencies**

The automated detection of archaeological topography is validated using stringent statistical metrics, principally the Intersection-over-Union (IoU) and the F1-score.8

* **Intersection-over-Union (IoU):** Measures the spatial overlap between the model's predicted bounding polygon and the human-annotated ground truth mask. An IoU score exceeding 0.5 is the accepted standard threshold for a "true positive" prediction in archaeological remote sensing.8  
* **Precision vs. Recall Tuning:** Due to the severe, irreversible consequences of missing an archaeological site prior to modern infrastructural development or destructive logging, archaeological machine learning models are deliberately tuned to prioritize *recall* over precision.37 Exploratory survey models frequently generate ground-truth ratios of 2.5 to 3.2, deliberately accepting a higher volume of false positives (e.g., classifying a modern logging track as an ancient Roman road, or a natural tree-throw as a burial pit) to ensure that no genuine heritage feature is overlooked during the prospection phase.37

However, the primary developmental bottleneck in the field remains the scarcity of large, high-quality, ground-truthed training datasets.4 Models perform exceptionally well on morphologically homogeneous features—such as the tens of thousands of identical charcoal burning platforms mapped in Baden-Württemberg 4—but struggle significantly to generalize across highly degraded, amorphous, or multi-period structures where the visual signature is heavily obscured by thousands of years of erosion.

## **Conclusions: Genuine Utility vs. Academic Curiosity**

The proliferation of advanced visualization algorithms between 2018 and 2025 has provided researchers with unparalleled computational tools for landscape interrogation. However, an honest, critical assessment of the state of the art requires separating algorithms of genuine, field-tested archaeological utility from those that serve primarily as mathematical or geomatics curiosities.  
**Algorithms of Genuine Archaeological Utility:**

1. **Combined VAT (Visualization for Archaeological Topography):** This is unquestionably the most robust, universally applicable algorithm developed and standardized in the last decade. Its ability to render intuitive, artifact-free baseline maps across both flat alluvial plains and steep upland karst makes it absolutely essential for standard publication, cross-regional comparison, and open-data sharing.6  
2. **Simple Red Relief / RRIM Variants:** These models are essential for macro-scale surveys in densely forested tropical environments. The chromatic separation of slope (red) and openness (brightness) allows the human eye to parse enormous landscapes instantly, serving as the unquestioned backbone for Mesoamerican and Southeast Asian survey workflows where massive data footprints require rapid triage.11  
3. **Local Dominance (LD) & Topographic Openness:** These are mathematically clean, highly targeted metrics. Openness provides unparalleled edge-detection capabilities that are crucial for CNN feature engineering, while LD remains unmatched for isolating near-invisible, leveled anomalies in European agricultural contexts.14  
4. **e4MSTP:** As the final iteration of the enhanced MSTP suite, e4MSTP is a masterclass in multi-scalar data fusion. By abandoning the patented RRIM components and fusing multi-radius SVFs with Openness and Slope, it provides an unparalleled view of complex, multi-period landscapes on flat terrain.13

**Algorithms Primarily Representing Academic Curiosities:**

1. **Enhanced Prismatic Openness:** While theoretically sound in its fusion techniques and its abandonment of vertical exaggeration, the vivid saturation and highly unnatural color matrices produced by this method can be visually jarring and difficult to interpret over prolonged periods. In practice, analysts overwhelmingly favor VAT for general readability or RRIM for structural extraction, leaving Prismatic Openness largely confined to experimental case studies.3  
2. **e2MSTP and e3MSTP:** These algorithms were essentially intermediary evolutionary steps. Developed primarily to circumvent the RRIM patent by utilizing complex color blends (CRIM), they take substantial time for the human eye to learn and interpret. They have been largely superseded in the literature and in practice by the mathematically superior, patent-free e4MSTP.13

Moving forward, the standardization of metadata and algorithm parameter documentation is paramount. As archaeological remote sensing transitions fully into the era of deep learning, the precision, reproducibility, and mathematical consistency of open-source tools—such as the algorithms integrated within QGIS plugins and the Relief Visualization Toolbox (RVT)—will serve as the foundational bedrock upon which automated, planetary-scale heritage mapping is built.
